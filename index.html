My Principles

Accept your weaknesses and build systems to simply manage them.

Read. Everything you want to do has been done before.

Understand opportunity cost. Say no to most things.

Pursue the best learning opportunities first. The money will follow.

Life will be hard work. You just choose when.

If problems reoccur, you haven't learned the lesson yet. Analyze the root cause.

Motivation and will power are fickle. Use habits to your advantage.

Don't decide what you want. Decide what you are willing to struggle for.

Above all, know yourself and act on that knowledge.

Managing Oneself by Peter Drucker

Knowing yourself (your strengths/weaknesses, how you perform, what your values are, etc) is the simplest way to succeed. Because everybody is different, only after you understand how YOU work will you be able to put yourself in the best position to take advantage of your strengths. This ability to “hack yourself” is the difference between thriving and trying to keep your head above water. This book is also where I learned to adopt the approach: “Don’t try to improve your weaknesses. Instead, work to improve your strengths.”



Principles by Ray Dalio

Most people treat every situation as if it’s completely unique, but the truth is most of them reoccur. Instead of responding randomly or based on feelings in the moment, Ray Dalio believes in having a set of principles that you use to make decisions. This systematic approach allows you to see in a cause-and-effect manner what works and what doesn’t. Reading this book has helped me uncover my own principles (as seen on this website) and improve my life in a logical manner.



I Will Teach You To Be Rich by Ramit Sethi

This is the only step-by-step guide that you need to take control of your finances because every chapter has actionable steps. His philosophy is to first take care of what you need to (retirement/savings) so that you can spend money on the things you love, guilt-free. Everything in his system is automatic so you never leave your finances to chance, or worse, will power. I personally am a big fan because he doesn’t try to preach the “latte factor” (eliminating a latte a day to save up money) and cutting out “luxuries”; instead, he just wants prioritize which luxuries.



Think Like a Freak by Steven Levitt and Stephen Dubner

The authors of the Freakonomics series explain how thinking differently is all it takes to solve seemingly unsolvable problems. With an emphasis on questioning what you know, they show that it’s more important to ask the right questions than to get the right answers. This approach gives people the tools to constantly improve and excel. With interesting examples like why scammers from Nigeria always say they’re from Nigeria and why rockstars ask for a bowl of only green M&M’s, this fun and easy read convinced me that saying “I don’t know” is okay and is the reason I became comfortable with asking questions.



Rich Dad Poor Dad by Robert Kiyosaki

If you want to read to learn how to better your life, this book is the turning-point for just that. It made me realize that having a high-paying job isn’t enough to be financially independent and was a huge wake-up call for me. This book provides the foundation you need for personal finance because it completely changes how you view money. It’s also written for everyday people so even people who don’t like to read will read this book and say it changed their life.



The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F*ck by Mark Manson

Every other self help book tries to get you to feel good, but Mark Manson is brutally honest and doesn’t care about your feelings. He forces you to face the ugly parts of your life so you can actually do something to change them. It’s the only personal development book that will give you the “kick in the butt” that you need to clarify your priorities and decide what you will and will not put up with. This is also where I learned to adopt the approach “Don’t decide what you want. Decide what you’re willing to struggle for”.
